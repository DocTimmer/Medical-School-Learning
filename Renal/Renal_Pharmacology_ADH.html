The Breakdown: V1 vs V2 Physiology

1. Receptor Selectivity is Everything

To master these drugs for the boards, you must separate the receptors. V1 receptors live on vascular smooth muscle and cause intense vasoconstriction. V2 receptors live on the basolateral membrane of the collecting duct principal cells and trigger AQP2 insertion to reabsorb free water. Desmopressin (DDAVP) is a surgical strike on V2—perfect for treating Central DI or nocturnal enuresis without spiking the patient's blood pressure. It also serendipitously causes endothelial cells to release vWF and Factor VIII, making it useful for bleeding disorders.

2. The Shock State

Vasopressin is the sledgehammer. Because it's non-selective, it hits V1 receptors to clamp down blood vessels. We use this mechanism to our advantage in distributive/septic shock to maintain perfusion when norepinephrine isn't enough, or to constrict splanchnic vessels in esophageal variceal bleeding. The major risk? Clamping down too hard and causing mesenteric or coronary ischemia.

3. The "Vaptans" and Rapid Sodium Correction

The antagonists (Tolvaptan, Conivaptan) force the kidneys to dump pure, electrolyte-free water (aquaresis). This is brilliant for hypervolemic or euvolemic hyponatremia (SIADH). However, the absolute highest-yield board concept here is the speed of sodium correction. If you clear the water too fast, sodium spikes rapidly, ripping water out of brain cells and causing Osmotic Demyelinating Syndrome (ODS)—formerly known as Central Pontine Myelinolysis. Never correct sodium faster than 6–8 mEq/L per 24 hours.

4. Demeclocycline

This is a classic step 1 quirk. It's a tetracycline antibiotic that happens to interfere with the ADH signaling cascade at the collecting duct, inducing a localized nephrogenic diabetes insipidus. It's an older drug used for chronic SIADH.
